The Analytical Language Engine

Being a candid mechanical account of the Large Language Model — what truly happens between your question and its answer — rendered in brass, in the manner of Mr. Charles Babbage.

Everything below actually runs: the gauges are moved by a real (if miniature) engine of probability computed live on this page. The plaques state, candidly, how the full-sized article differs.

The Operator’s Desk

To operate: 1 — write a few opening words upon the card, or take a ready-punched card from the rack; 2 — pull Begin the Demonstration and follow the works, pressing Proceed at each station; 3 — thereafter Turn the Crank for each further word, or let the engine Run. Mind the boiler.

Station I

The Card Reader

Fig. 1 — the prompt
Feed slot · one card, seven holes the column
the engine
your words, as holes — seven bits the column
The card lies ready in the slot.

Your words are punched upon a card and fed to the reader. The engine does not hear you — it receives marks.

In truth — a language model receives nothing but text: no voice, no view of the world, no notion of who is asking beyond what the words themselves carry. Everything that follows is determined by these characters and by the settings of the machine’s parts.

A note on the manner of address. To a chat engine you write whole questions; this cabinet asks instead for a few opening words to continue. There is no contradiction — a chat engine is a continuation engine too: the clerks lay the conversation out as a transcript (Operator: … Engine: —) and the machine simply continues the document, which lands, every time, precisely upon the engine’s reply. The instructions that govern its manners — the system prompt — are more cards still, read before yours.

Station II

The Type-Cutting Works

Fig. 2 — tokenisation
The rack · every slug bears its catalogue number
rack empty — 0 of 24 places filled

The card is sheared into slugs — standard pieces from the engine’s type-case. Each slug bears a catalogue number, and from here onward the engine handles numbers only.

In truth — text is broken into tokens from a fixed catalogue of roughly 50,000–200,000 pieces. Common words are one token; rarer words are cut into fragments (un·fathom·able). The machine never sees letters or words as you do — only these numbers.

This cabinet’s catalogue holds — slugs; a slug marked ✶ was cut fresh and is a stranger to it. The rack holds only 24 slugs — its context window. Push in more and the oldest fall off the far end, forgotten. (A modern engine’s rack holds 200,000 or more.)

Station III

The Ledger of Meanings

Fig. 3 — embeddings
Specimens from the ledger · a fixed exhibit
The slugs at hand, exchanged for dial-columns
— awaiting slugs —
Note how ENGINE and GEARS set their dials nearly alike, while FOG keeps a different posture.

Each catalogue number is looked up in the Great Ledger, and the slug is exchanged for a column of dial-settings — its position upon the map of meaning.

In truth — every token becomes an embedding: a list of thousands of numbers (this cabinet shows eight dials; a large engine uses more than ten thousand). Nobody wrote the ledger by hand — the settings were learned, and they place words used in similar ways at similar settings. Meaning, to the machine, is position.

Station IV

The Linkage of Regards

Fig. 4 — attention
Two banks of linkages · thickness is regard
— awaiting slugs —
the RECENCY head (above)the AFFINITY head (below)

Here is the celebrated mechanism. Every slug is joined to every earlier slug by an adjustable linkage; the engine draws some taut and lets others hang slack. A word discovers what it means by choosing what to regard.

In truth — this is attention, the invention (2017) that makes these engines possible. For each position the model computes how much every earlier token should bear upon it — how it finds its noun, how bank leans toward the river or the counting-house. Dozens of heads work in parallel, each minding a different sort of relation: shown here, a brass head that favours the recent, a copper head that favours old acquaintances.

Depicted schematically — in the full engine these weights are computed afresh, at every layer, from the dial-settings themselves.

Station V

The Grand Mill

Fig. 5 — layers & training
Cross-section · six floors of a hundred · the bars trace one column’s settings
The mill stands ready.

The columns now descend through the Mill — floor after identical floor of linkage-and-gearwork, each refining the settings handed down by the last.

In truth — attention plus a small calculating network make one layer, and layers are stacked by the dozen (a large model, near a hundred). A token’s numbers are revised at every floor — coarse matters of spelling and grammar low down, subtler matters of sense and intent above, or so the anatomists report. The adjustable parts — the parameters — number in the hundreds of billions, and none was set by hand: in training, the engine read a vast library, guessed each next token, and every screw was turned a hair’s-breadth whenever it guessed wrong. Repeated a very great many times, that is the whole of its education. A finishing school follows — training on human preference — to make it helpful, honest, and polite.

This cabinet, for comparison, owns — counting-wheels, learned from a library of — sentences.

Station VI

The Manometer Wall

Fig. 6 — the probability board
Live gauges · the seven strongest candidates, and the rest pooled
— the gauges stand at rest —

At the mill’s outfall the engine renders its judgment — not a word, but a pressure upon every gauge in the house: a score for every slug in the catalogue at once.

In truth — the model’s output at each step is a probability for every token in its vocabulary — all 50,000-odd, most of them minuscule. It does not decide the next word; it rates all possible ones. These gauges are live: they show this engine’s true arithmetic for the present context — and they obey the boiler lever at Station VII, should you care to move it.

Station VII

The Governor & the Lottery Drum

Fig. 7 — temperature & sampling
TEMPERATE · T = 0.70
The lottery drum · lots cast in proportion to the gauges
No lot has yet been drawn.

One slug must be drawn. The lots are cast in proportion to the gauges — and the boiler decides how strictly. Run cold, the favourite is all but certain; run feverish, and outsiders take their chance.

In truth — the next token is chosen by weighted lottery — sampling — and temperature reshapes the odds exactly as shown: low temperature sharpens the distribution toward the favourite (predictable, sometimes repetitious); high temperature flattens it (inventive, and at the extreme, incoherent). This is why the same question, asked twice, may be answered differently. Try the lever — the gauges obey at once.

Station VIII

The Endless Crank

Fig. 8 — the loop, and stopping
The tape · and the crank that turns it all again
❧
REVOLUTION № 000
faint ink — your card · dark ink — minted by the engine · ∎ — the stop token

The chosen slug is stamped upon the tape — and carried straight back to the rack, where it joins your question. The whole engine then turns over again, from card to lottery, to choose the word after. One revolution, one word.

In truth — a language model writes autoregressively: one token per pass, each pass re-reading all that came before. There is no finished answer waiting inside; each word is minted the moment before you read it. And the engine stops as it speaks — by predicting a special stop token (printed here as ∎), whereupon it rests.

The full engine keeps notes — a cache — so as not to redo old arithmetic on every revolution; but in principle, every turn reads the whole rack afresh.

A candid note, screwed to the cabinet.

This cabinet is honest about being small. Its probabilities come from a genuinely working but miniature engine — a counting of word-patterns (a Markov chain) over a little library of — sentences — so that real arithmetic, not pantomime, moves the gauges. A modern LLM produces its probabilities not by counting but by the learned machinery of Stations III–V, and its scale is of another order: a catalogue of ~50,000–200,000 tokens against our — · a rack of ~200,000 slugs against our 24 · ~100 floors against our pretended 6 · parameters in the hundreds of billions against our — wheels. But the pipeline itself — cut to slugs, look up the dials, regard the context, refine by floors, gauge every candidate, draw the lot, and crank again — is the honest shape of the thing.

Post-scriptum, on the question of building it. A correspondent engine of our acquaintance has computed what a full-sized specimen would come to, wrought honestly in brass at one counting-column to each parameter. At a million million parts — the present fashion — and with decent aisles for the oilers, the works would cover the Principality of Wales, near enough entire. Every mill labouring together at Mr. Babbage’s own pace, it might mint one word in a season, and a civil reply in a generation. Its boilers would want half the coal of Britain, and its frames more brass than the world of 1843 had ever smelted. The arithmetic is left in the margin for the sceptical.

The margin — area: 10¹² parameters × one five-wheel column at a hand’s-breadth square, (0.1 m)², ≈ 10,000 km²; doubled for aisles and shafting ≈ Wales, 20,779 km². Pace: ~2×10¹² multiply-accumulations the token, shared among two million mills at ten seconds the operation ≈ four months the word. Coal: those mills and their line-shafting, steam-driven at Victorian efficiency ≈ tens of millions of tons the year, Britain’s whole cut of 1850 being about sixty million. Brass: ~½ kg the column ≈ 500 million tons — centuries of smelting, as reckoned from 1843. The cabinet before you, by contrast, travels in a web page.

Post-post-scriptum, on the labour question. A Correspondent of sterner economy asks what the treadwheel might do that the boiler does now — the destitute and the orphan turning the works. The arithmetic is short and shameful: to replace the boilers would want some forty million souls in relays — half again the population of these islands — upon eight hundred and fifty thousand wheels; every pauper actually on the parish books, treading day and night, would hasten each four-month word by four days; and the orphans at their cranks would buy an hour. Bread, moreover, runs thirty times dearer than coal, watt for watt — which is why the age yoked its wheels to punishment, and often to nothing at all. The Committee declines the scheme entire, and is pleased to observe that in this it anticipates Parliament by some fifty-five years.

The margin — demand: ~1 GW at the prime movers, as computed above. A treader: ~60 W for a ten-hour day; a child at the crank, ~15 W. Replacement: 10⁹ ÷ 60 ≈ 17 million treading at every hour, ×2.4 for relays ≈ 40 million; ÷ 20 places the wheel ≈ 850,000 wheels, whose sheds ≈ 42 km² — a five-hundredth part of Wales, the scheme’s one economy. As found: Britain’s ~1.5 million paupers yield ~37 MW ≈ 4 per cent ≈ four days the word; its ~65,000 workhouse orphans ~0.4 MW ≈ an hour. The ledger: a pauper’s keep, ~5d the day, buys ~2 MJ of work; a ton of coal at 10s buys 1.5 GJ — near thirty-fold the value. The penal wheel was struck off by the Prison Act of 1898; the treadmill returned, unaccountably, as a subscription service.
